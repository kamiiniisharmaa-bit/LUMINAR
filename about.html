The origin of LUMINAIR

Founded in 2018 between a small Stockholm studio and a sun-drenched atelier in Oaxaca, LUMINAIR was born from a quiet rebellion against mass production. Our founder, Elin Sörman, an architect and ceramicist, imagined a brand where each object carries a memory — of earth, of hands, of unhurried time.

What started as a collection of hand-thrown incense holders grew into a global community of artisans, designers, and seekers of ethereal beauty. Today, LUMINAIR collaborates with master craftspeople in Japan, Mexico, and Portugal, merging ancient techniques with contemporary minimalism.

— Slow is the new luminous.

Materials with memory

We source only organic, unaltered materials: raw alabaster that glows from within, hand‑dug clays from Kanayama, linen woven on traditional looms. Each object bears the fingerprint of its maker — subtle variations are never flaws, but the very signature of life.

LUMINAIR pieces are meant to be held, used, and aged with grace. We believe in wabi-sabi, the Japanese philosophy of finding beauty in imperfection and transience. A slight crack, a gentle unevenness — these are not hidden but celebrated.

Our packaging is 100% plastic-free, crafted from recycled fibers and wildflower seed paper. Because the ritual begins the moment you open the box.

Ateliers without borders

We work with family-owned workshops that have passed down skills for generations. In Kyoto, master woodworker Kenji-san carves our sculptural trays. In the hills of Oaxaca, the collective "Manos de Tierra" hand-dyes our linen pillows with indigo and cochineal.

Every LUMINAIR purchase directly supports fair wages, safe working conditions, and the preservation of endangered crafts. We are proud members of the Fair Trade Federation and publish our supply chain openly.

Together, we form a constellation of makers — each piece a quiet ambassador of its origin.

✦ Meet Mizuki Tanaka — ceramic whisperer ✦

“Clay is alive. It remembers the rain, the mountain, the hands that shaped it.” Mizuki is the fourth-generation potter in her family from Tajimi, Japan. For LUMINAIR, she crafts the 'Nebula' collection — vessels that hold incense and silence.

Her studio sits beside a cedar forest, where she uses only local clay and ash glazes made from rice husks. Each piece is fired in a single-chamber anagama kiln for three days. The result? Surfaces that seem to breathe.

“I love that LUMINAIR’s clients touch the pieces with reverence. It’s a dialogue across continents.”
